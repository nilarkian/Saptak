Definitions, Glossary & Ontology

Formal definitions · A–Z glossary · Concept relationships & domain diagrams

👥 Core Entities — The 5 Participants
Member
Policy Holder / Patient
Entity
The US individual covered by a health insurance policy — the patient who receives care. Every other entity in the system exists in relation to the member's healthcare journey. The member's personal and clinical data is classified as PHI and protected under HIPAA.
Provider
Healthcare Provider (Individual or Facility)
Entity
Any individual or organization that delivers medical services to a member. Individual providers (PCP, Specialist) bill with their NPI. Facility providers (Hospital, SNF, ASC, Home Health, Hospice) bill under a facility NPI. Providers may be in-network (participating — contracted with the payer) or out-of-network (non-participating).
Payer
Insurance Company / Health Plan
Entity
The entity that assumes financial responsibility for a member's medical costs — the insurance company. The payer receives claims from providers, adjudicates them (pays, partially pays, or denies), issues an EOB, and posts payment. In COB scenarios there can be a primary, secondary, and tertiary payer.
Supplier
Medical Equipment / Supply Vendor
Entity
Organizations that supply Durable Medical Equipment (DME) and medical supplies used to treat or prevent illness. Suppliers bill separately from providers using HCPCS codes. Examples: wheelchair vendors, prosthetics suppliers, home oxygen providers. Covered under Medicare Part B.
Researcher
Medical Researcher / Clinical Investigator
Entity
Individuals and organizations conducting experiments and studies for human health improvement. They touch PHI under research exemptions in HIPAA and must follow IRB guidelines. Not directly involved in billing but part of the HIPAA-regulated ecosystem through Business Associate Agreements (BAAs).
🔒 Privacy Law — HIPAA & PHI
HIPAA
Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, 1996
Law
A federal privacy law enacted in 1996 by DHHS that mandates confidentiality of all member health information. Four entity types must comply: Healthcare Providers, Health Care Plans (insurers), Health Care Clearing Houses (HCCH), and Business Associates (third-party vendors). Violations carry civil and criminal penalties.
PHI
Protected Health Information
Law
Any individually identifiable health information that must stay confidential. PHI comprises 18 identifiers: Name · DOB · SSN · MRN · Address / Zip · Phone & contact details · Photograph · Admission date · Discharge date · Date of death · Account numbers · Device identifiers · URLs · IP addresses · Biometric data · Full-face photos · Geolocation · Any other unique identifier.
HCCH
Health Care Clearing House
HIPAA Entity
Organizations that act as intermediaries between providers and payers by converting non-standardized billing data into standard EDI formats (e.g. 837P for professional claims). They are covered entities under HIPAA and must protect PHI handled during data conversion.
💳 Health Care Plans
Medicare
Federal Health Insurance Program
Gov Plan
A federally administered health insurance program covering individuals who are: (1) age 65+, (2) disabled and receiving Social Security benefits for 24+ months, (3) have paid 40 quarters of federal taxes, or (4) have ESRD. Covers 80% of allowed charges. Structured as four Parts: A (hospital), B (outpatient/DME), C (Advantage), D (drugs). Major payers: Humana, Cigna, UHC, BCBS, Aetna.
Medicaid
Joint State-Federal Health Program
Gov Plan
Jointly funded by state and federal governments, supervised by state government, monitored by federal. Covers 100% of costs for low-income individuals, the disabled, pregnant women, children, and anyone below the federal poverty line. No age limit. Operates on an HMO structure. Is the payer of last resort — always pays after all other insurers in a COB scenario.
HMO
Health Maintenance Organization
Managed Care
A managed care plan requiring members to choose a Primary Care Physician (PCP) who acts as the gatekeeper. Referrals are mandatory for specialist visits. Members must use in-network providers only — OON care is not covered except in emergencies. Cost-share is the lowest of the four plan types (~60/40 copay structure). Medicaid operates as an HMO.
PPO
Preferred Provider Organization
Managed Care
A managed care plan offering maximum flexibility — no PCP required, no referral needed to see specialists, and out-of-network providers are allowed (at higher cost). Members pay lower cost-share for in-network services and higher for OON. Verbal referrals are acceptable. The most expensive plan type in premiums but the most flexible in access.
EPO
Exclusive Provider Organization
Managed Care
Like an HMO without the PCP gatekeeper — no referral needed for specialists, but members are strictly in-network only. Unlike PPO, EPO will never cover OON services under any circumstance (not even at higher cost). Verbal referrals acceptable. EPO sits between HMO and PPO in cost.
POS
Point of Service Plan
Managed Care
An HMO with a limited OON escape hatch. Requires a PCP referral for all specialist visits; the PCP referral can unlock OON coverage at higher cost. Without the referral, OON care is not covered. Cost-share mirrors HMO for in-network, higher for OON. Effectively a hybrid: HMO discipline plus limited PPO-style flexibility through the PCP.
TRICARE
Formerly CHAMPUS — Civilian Health And Medical Program of the Uniformed Services
Gov Program
Health insurance for military personnel — active duty, deceased, disabled, and retired service members — plus their dependents (heirs). Administered by the Department of Defense. Covers the same spectrum as civilian insurance (medical, prescriptions, mental health).
CHAMPVA
Civilian Health And Medical Program of the Department of Veterans Affairs
Gov Program
A health-benefit program covering medical costs for war veterans — active, deceased, disabled, retired, or partially disabled during war — and their eligible dependents. Administered by the Veterans Affairs (VA). TRICARE covers current military; CHAMPVA covers veterans and their families.
🧑‍⚕️ Healthcare Providers
PCP
Primary Care Physician
Individual
The member's first point of contact and the system's gatekeeper. The PCP diagnoses, treats, and coordinates care. Under HMO and POS plans, the PCP must issue a Referral (the first-time authorization) before the member can see a specialist. CPT range for PCP office visits: 9920x.
Specialist
Specialty Physician (Referred by PCP)
Individual
A physician with advanced training in a specific area (cardiology, orthopedics, oncology, etc.). Referred to by the PCP under managed care plans. The specialist then requests further Authorization from the payer for any procedures or surgeries. CPT range: 9921x. The specialist is the Servicing/Rendering provider on a PA form (Section B).
SNF
Skilled Nursing Facility
Facility
A recovery center providing post-surgical skilled nursing care plus Activities of Daily Living (ADL): bathing, dressing, cooking, occupational therapy. Distinct from a hospital (acute care) — SNF is for recovery and rehabilitation. Covered under Medicare Part A for eligible inpatient stays.
ASC
Ambulatory Surgical Center
Facility
A same-day surgical center — the patient is admitted, has the procedure, and is discharged on the same calendar day. Handles short, lower-risk procedures (e.g. cataract surgery, colonoscopy, arthroscopy). POS code 24 = ASC. Covered under Medicare Part B as an outpatient facility.
Hospice
End-of-Life Care Program
Facility
A specialized care program for terminally ill individuals in their final stages, focused on comfort rather than curative treatment. Care is delivered at home or in home-like settings. Medicare covers hospice under Part A when a physician certifies a life expectancy of 6 months or less. Family caregivers are also supported.
🔢 Medical Coding System
CPT
Current Procedural Terminology
Code Set
Published by the AMA. 5-digit numeric codes describing every medical procedure, service, or treatment a provider can perform. The core billing code — without CPT, a claim cannot be processed. Two key E/M ranges: 99202–99205 (new patient), 99212–99215 (established patient). Higher code = more time/complexity.
ICD / ICD-10
International Classification of Disease
Code Set
An alphanumeric code set representing the patient's diagnosis — the why of the visit. ICD-10 is the current revision. Maintained by WHO. Every claim must include at least one ICD code to justify the CPT procedure (medical necessity). Example: R51 = Headache, E11.22 = Type 2 diabetes with chronic kidney disease.
HCPCS
Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System
Code Set
5-character alphanumeric codes for items not covered by CPT — primarily DME, supplies, drugs, and ambulance services. Level I = CPT codes (maintained by AMA). Level II = HCPCS codes (maintained by CMS). Example: E0265 = Hospital bed, A0021 = Ambulance, A5500 = Diabetic shoes.
Modifier
CPT (Level 1) or HCPCS (Level 2) Add-on Code
Code Set
2-character alphanumeric code appended to a CPT or HCPCS code to provide additional clinical context without changing the code's definition. Level 1 (CPT) modifiers are numeric (e.g. 50 = bilateral, 51 = multiple procedures). Level 2 (HCPCS) modifiers are alpha (e.g. RT = right side, LT = left side).
POS Code
Place of Service Code
Code Set
2-digit numeric code on a claim indicating where the service was physically rendered. Critical for reimbursement — the same CPT code pays differently in a hospital vs. an office. Key codes: 11 = Office, 12 = Home, 21 = Inpatient Hospital, 22 = Outpatient Hospital, 24 = ASC, 31 = SNF.
📋 Claims, Billing & Adjudication
Authorization
Prior Authorization (PA) — Payer Permission
Process
A designated permission a provider must obtain from the payer before performing a high-cost or complex service. Obtained via: call · portal · PA form · e-fax. The first authorization is a Referral (PCP → Insurance). Subsequent authorizations are for specific services. Missing authorization = automatic denial. Auth has a TAT like claims.
Adjudication
Claim Evaluation & Decision Process
Process
The automated or manual process a payer uses to evaluate a submitted claim and determine the outcome. Three sequential gates: (1) Filed within TFL? (2) Insurance active on DOS? (3) Authorization present? If all pass → adjudication → payment or partial payment. Outcome documented in the EOB sent to both provider and member.
EOB
Explanation of Benefits
Document
A detailed summary sent by the payer to both the provider and the member after a claim is adjudicated. EOB is NOT a bill. It shows: service rendered · amount billed · amount allowed · amount paid · patient responsibility remaining · denial reason (if applicable). The EOB is the source of truth for AR reconciliation.
Appeal
Formal Reconsideration Request
Process
A formal request submitted by either the provider or the member asking the health plan to reverse a denied claim or authorization. Two outcomes: (1) Reversed — plan overturns the denial, claim is paid. (2) Upheld — denial stands, balance may be written off to AR. Both parties have the right to appeal.
TFL & TAT
Timely Filing Limit & Turn Around Time
Timeline
TFL is the deadline set by the insurance plan within which a provider must submit a claim — missing it causes automatic denial (Reason 1 of 3 classic denials). TAT is the time the adjudication team takes to decide on a claim or authorization. Both are set by the individual payer. US Calendar Year resets on 01/01.
COB
Coordination of Benefits
Process
The process of determining which payer pays first (primary), second (secondary), or third (tertiary) when a member holds multiple insurance plans simultaneously. Prevents double-payment. Order: Medicare → commercial → Medicaid (last resort). Classic dual member scenario: Medicare primary + Medicaid secondary → patient pays ~$0.
💰 Financial Models & Patient Responsibility
Capitation
Per Head Per Month (PHPM) Payment Model
Payment Model
A fixed income model where the payer pays the provider a set amount per member per month regardless of how many visits the member makes. Incentivizes keeping members healthy (fewer visits = same pay). Expressed as $/member/month. Typical in HMO plans. Contrast with FFS where more visits = more revenue.
FFS
Fee for Service
Payment Model
A payment model where providers are paid separately for each service rendered — every procedure billed individually in an unbundled format. More services = more revenue. Incentivizes volume over outcomes. The traditional model in US healthcare. Contrasts with capitation's flat retainer structure.
Deductible (PR-1)
Annual Out-of-Pocket Threshold
Patient Resp.
A fixed annual dollar amount the member must pay entirely out-of-pocket before the insurance starts sharing costs. The member pays 100% until this "bucket" is full. Resets every calendar year (Jan 1 → Dec 31). Known at plan enrollment. Once met, co-insurance kicks in. Example: $2,000 deductible = member pays first $2,000 of claims each year.
Co-Insurance (PR-2)
Percentage Cost-Share
Patient Resp.
The percentage split between the member and the insurer on allowed charges — activates after the deductible is met. The exact dollar amount is not known until the claim processes. Example: 80/20 split on a $1,000 claim → insurer pays $800, member pays $200. The member's share is PR-2.
Co-Pay (PR-3)
Fixed Per-Visit Fee
Patient Resp.
A fixed dollar amount the member pays at the time of service — independent of what was done and regardless of deductible status. Paid upfront before the claim is processed. Example: $30 co-pay for every specialist visit. Co-pay does not count toward the co-insurance calculation but does count toward the OOP ceiling.
OOP
Out of Pocket Maximum
Patient Resp.
The total annual ceiling on member spending: OOP = Deductible + Co-Insurance + Co-Pay + Non-Covered Charges. Once the OOP maximum is reached, the insurance covers 100% of remaining covered costs for the rest of the calendar year. Resets Jan 1. Protects members from catastrophic out-of-pocket spending.
