    📋 What this covers

    Money moves through US healthcare along one chain: a member gets sick → a provider treats them → a payer foots the bill → coders translate the visit into numbers → billers fight to get paid. These notes walk that chain end-to-end — who the players are, what protects the patient (HIPAA), how plans are structured (Medicare/Medicaid), and how a claim actually gets coded, billed, and split between insurer and patient.

    🔒 HIPAA & PHI

    A patient's medical life is private by law, not by courtesy. That law is HIPAA, and the data it guards is PHI.

    HIPAA

    ℹ️ HIPAA — Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act

    A federal privacy law that forces all members' personally identifiable health information to stay confidential and private.

    • 📅 Enacted 1996
    • 🏛️ By DHHS (Department of Health and Human Services)
    • It is a privacy law/rule for an act which ensures that all the members health information are kept confidential with utmost security and privacy

    Four entities must follow HIPAA guidelines:

    = Insurance Companies] H --> C[HCCH
    Health Care Clearing House] H --> D[Business Associates]
    EntityOne-line role
    Health Care ProvidersIndividuals/orgs giving medical services (doctors, hospitals) — keep member details confidential.
    Health Care PlansThe insurance policies covering members' medical expenses — another name for the insurance company.
    HCCH (Health Care Clearing House)Orgs that process the data used for billing. orgs that standardize the non-standardized data which are used for billing

    Medicare vs Medicaid

    The fastest way to keep them straight: Medicare = age/disability (federal), Medicaid = poverty (state + federal).

    🟦 MEDICARE🟩 MEDICAID

    📌 Two extra Medicaid facts buried in the notes
    • It's the last payer to pay when a member has multiple coverages (the "payer of last resort").
    • Also covers ESRD patients — End Stage Renal Disease.
    📌 Cross-reference: Medicaid = HMO

    Medicaid operates on an HMO structure — members are locked into a network, referrals are required, and no OON coverage. Knowing this makes HMO plan rules immediately applicable to Medicaid scenarios.

    Government Programs

    Beyond Medicare/Medicaid, four programs cover special groups:

    TRICARE

    Formerly CHAMPUS (Civilian Health And Medical Program of the Uniformed Services). Health insurance for military personnel — active, deceased, disabled, retirees, and their dependents (heirs).

    CHAMPVA

    Civilian Health And Medical Program of the Department of Veterans Affairs. A health-benefit program covering care costs for war veterans — active, deceased, disabled, retiress, or partially disabled during war — and their dependents.

    No-Fault Liability (Third-Party Insurance)

    Pays for care resulting from injury to a person or damage to property in an accident — regardless of who caused it. (add on, optional)

    Workers' Compensation

    Covers injuries employees sustain on work premises (or elsewhere during active work hours).

    🩺 Components of Medicare

    Medicare isn't one thing — it's four parts (A/B/C/D), and the trick is mapping each to what it actually pays for.

    PartNicknameCoversEx.

    🛠️ DME — Durable Medical Equipment

    ℹ️ DME

    Medical equipment that can be rented or purchased for repeated home use. Examples: walkers, wheelchairs, hospital beds, prosthetic/orthotic supplies. (Billed under Part B.)

    Major Medicare payers (carry the "MCR" tag)

    Humana · Cigna · United Health Care (UHC) · BCBS · Aetna

    • The member should always visit in-network providers.

    📝 Authorization & Referral

    Before expensive care happens, the provider usually has to ask permission. That permission is Authorization.

    ⚠️ Authorization

    A designated permission a provider requests from the health care plan to get a medical service done on a member.

    • 🥇 The first-time authorization is called a Referral.

    The call flow:

    • PCP → Insurance = Referral call
    • Specialist → Insurance (for service)
    • Specialist → Insurance (for authorization) = Authorization call
    • Authorization → Doctor → Insurance

    4 ways to initiate an Authorization:

    1. ☎️ Call
    2. 💻 Portals
    3. 📄 P.A. Form (Prior Authorization form)
    4. 📠 E-Fax (Electronic Fax)

    💰 Payment Modules (2 types)

    How does a provider actually get paid? Two opposing philosophies — a flat retainer vs. pay-per-service.

    ModuleIncome typeHow it works
    Capitation🔒 Fixed incomeFollows PHPM (Per Head Per Month / Per Member Per Month, $/mt). Provider gets a set amount regardless of how many visits the member makes.
    Fee for Service (FFS)📈 Incentive typeEvery service is billed separately in an un-bundled format. More services = more money.
    💡 Why it matters

    Capitation rewards keeping people healthy (fewer visits = same pay). FFS rewards volume (more procedures = more pay). The incentive structure is the whole point.

    📋 Billing & Claims Concepts

    A claim is a clock. Two timers govern its life: how long you have to file, and how long the payer takes to decide.

    ℹ️ TFL — Timely Filing Limit

    The time frame within which a provider must submit a claim to the health plan. Set by the insurance itself. Miss it and the claim dies on arrival.

    ℹ️ TAT — Turn Around Time

    The time interval the adjudication team takes to decide whether to pay or deny a claim. (TAT also applies to Authorization.)

    Provider/claim identifiers

    IDFull formFormatShared?
    NPINational Provider Identifier10-digit uniqueOne per individual provider
    TINTax Identification Number9-digitShared by multiple providers

    📅 US Calendar Year: 01/01/20XX → 12/31/20XX — deductibles and OOP reset on this cycle.

    ⛔ 3 classic reasons for denial

    1. Untimely filing limit — claim filed after TFL.
    2. No active insurance for the Date of Service (DOS).
    3. Provider never generated an Authorization.
    📌 Appeal — Formal Reconsideration

    A formal reconsideration request submitted by a provider OR a member to a health care plan, asking them to reverse a denied claim or authorization.

    • Who can file: Provider or Member — both have the right.
    • Trigger: A denial — of a claim or of an authorization.
    • Outcomes: Plan Reverses → claim is paid  ·  Plan Upholds → denial stands.

    🗂️ Common Terminology

    A few acronyms recur in every claim conversation — lock these down.

    ℹ️ COB — Coordination of Benefits

    The process of determining which payer is the member's primary, secondary, or tertiary — i.e. who takes financial responsibility, and in what order. Exists because some members carry 2+ plans simultaneously — COB prevents double-payment and ensures the provider gets fully reimbursed.

    1. 🥇 Primary Payer — pays first (up to plan limits)
    2. 🥈 Secondary Payer — covers remainder from primary
    3. 🥉 Tertiary Payer — covers what secondary left
    4. 💳 Patient Responsibility — member pays any remaining balance

    Classic scenario: Medicare (primary) + Medicaid (secondary) → Medicare pays first, Medicaid covers the remainder → Member out-of-pocket ≈ $0. This member is a Dual Member (see below).

    ℹ️ EOB — Explanation of Benefits

    A detailed summary of a processed claim, shared by the insurance with both the provider and the member. EOB is NOT a bill — it is a statement of what happened after adjudication.

    EOB FieldWhat it shows
    Service renderedWhat procedure was billed
    Amount billedWhat the provider charged
    Amount allowedWhat the plan contractually allows
    Amount paidWhat insurance actually paid
    Patient responsibilityWhat the member owes
    Denial reasonWhy part or all was not paid (if applicable)
    ✅ Dual Members

    Members eligible for both government plans simultaneously: Medicare + Medicaid.

    • Who qualifies: Must satisfy both criteria at once — age 65+ or disabled (Medicare) and below the federal poverty line (Medicaid).
    • Payer order: Medicare = primary  ·  Medicaid = secondary (payer of last resort).
    • Out-of-pocket: Near-zero — Medicaid covers what Medicare doesn't pay.

    🧾 Patient Responsibility (PR)

    After the insurer pays its share, what's left lands on the patient — roughly ~20%. It comes in three flavors plus a ceiling.

    graph TD PR["Patient Responsibility (PR) ~20%"] --> PR1["Deductible (PR-1)"] PR --> PR2["Co-Insurance (PR-2)"] PR --> PR3["Co-Pay (PR-3)"]
    CodeTermWhat it isWhen paid
    PR-1DeductibleA fixed dollar amount the member must "finish off" first within a calendar year, after which insurance takes over.First, annually
    PR-2Co-InsuranceThe cost share between member and insurer, shown as a percentage. Actual $ known only after the claim is processed.After processing
    PR-3Co-PayA fixed dollar amount the member pays the provider up-front, before/at the time of service.Up-front
    ⚠️ OOP — Out of Pocket

    The total ceiling on member spending:

    OOP = Deductible (PR-1) + Co-Insurance (PR-2) + Co-Pay (PR-3) + Non-Covered Charges

    Once OOP is hit, the insurance covers the rest.

    Worked Examples

    📊 Co-Insurance example (80/20)

    Claim = $1,000 · Co-insurance = 80/20
    → Insurance pays $800 · Member pays $200.
    The member learns the dollar amount only after the claim processes.

    💊 Co-Pay example

    Every specialist visit = $30 co-pay — fixed, regardless of what was done. Paid upfront at time of service, independent of whether the deductible has been met.

    🏦 Deductible — "fill the bucket first"

    Until the deductible threshold is reached, the member pays 100% out-of-pocket. Once the bucket is full, insurance starts sharing cost. Resets every calendar year (Jan 1 → Dec 31).

    💡 If you remember nothing else
    • Medicare = 65+/federal/80% · Medicaid = poor/state+federal/100%/last payer
    • Part A hospital · Part B outpatient+DME · Part C = A+B+extras · Part D drugs · MAPD = C+D
    • HMO = referral + in-network only + cheap · PPO = no referral + OON allowed + pricey
    • CPT = treatment (5 numeric) · ICD = disease (alphanumeric) · HCPCS = equipment · POS = where · Modifiers = clarity
    • TFL = deadline to file · TAT = time to decide
    • NPI = 10-digit, one provider · TIN = 9-digit, shared
    • PR1 Deductible · PR2 Co-Insurance · PR3 Co-Pay · OOP = all PRs + non-covered
    • 3 denials: late filing · no active insurance · no authorization
    • PHI: name · DOB · SSN · MRN · address · phone · photos · admission/discharge/death dates
    • HIPAA: 1996 · DHHS · 4 entities must comply: providers · health plans · HCCH · business associates
    • EPO: no referral + INN only + no OON ever (absolute)
    • POS: referral + INN primary + limited OON with referral
    • COB: who pays first/second/third — primary → secondary → tertiary → patient
    • EOB: processed claim summary → sent to provider + member · NOT a bill
    • Dual Member: Medicare (primary) + Medicaid (secondary) simultaneously · OOP ≈ $0
